Problem Methodology Results

Hybrid SOS Trigger Analysis System

Improving reliability of automatic SOS trigger mechanisms in wearable safety systems using temporal validation and multi-condition motion analysis.

Problem Statement

Wearable SOS systems rely on acceleration threshold-based triggering, which fails to distinguish between normal motion and emergency situations. Activities such as running or jumping generate short-duration spikes that exceed thresholds, causing false alerts. These repeated false triggers reduce reliability and may lead to alarm fatigue, decreasing response effectiveness. Therefore, there is a need for improving detection accuracy without increasing hardware complexity in embedded systems.

Literature Review

Mubashir et al. (2013) conducted a comprehensive survey on fall detection systems used primarily in healthcare applications such as elderly monitoring in hospitals and assisted living environments. Their study highlighted that threshold-based detection methods are widely used due to their simplicity and low computational cost. However, they reported that these systems produce high false positives during daily activities like running, jumping, or sudden movement. Li et al. (2019) proposed improvements in wearable monitoring systems by introducing temporal filtering. Their work demonstrated that considering the duration of abnormal acceleration significantly improves detection accuracy, as real fall events exhibit sustained motion rather than isolated spikes. Other advanced approaches using machine learning and sensor fusion have been applied in medical monitoring systems to improve accuracy. However, these solutions require higher computational power, memory, and training data, making them unsuitable for low-cost embedded safety devices. This project adapts these research findings from healthcare systems to personal safety applications. It introduces a lightweight multi-condition detection mechanism combining duration validation, inactivity detection, and repeated spike analysis to improve reliability without increasing hardware complexity.

Research Gap

Most embedded SOS systems still rely on simple threshold-based triggering and do not validate motion over time. They fail to distinguish between transient spikes and sustained abnormal motion. This project addresses this gap by introducing a structured multi-layer detection mechanism suitable for embedded deployment.

System Methodology

Dataset

Simulated accelerometer data representing walking, running, jumping, fall, and irregular motion patterns.

Model

Detection begins with threshold validation and is enhanced using duration-based filtering to ensure only sustained abnormal motion triggers alerts.

Advanced Logic

Further validation includes inactivity detection after impact and repeated spike pattern analysis to detect unstable or distress motion conditions.

The system compares threshold-only detection with duration-based and multi-condition validation approaches to evaluate improvements in reliability and reduction in false triggers.

Results & Analysis

Threshold-only detection produced false alerts during short-duration spikes such as jumping. Duration-based filtering eliminated these false positives. Additional validation using inactivity and repeated spike patterns further improved detection accuracy and reliability.
